Senior Project

Drowsiness Detection

Each year, drowsy driving accounts for about 100,000 crashes, 71,000 injuries and 1,550 fatalities, according to the National Safety Council (NSC). The real number may be much higher, however, as it is difficult to determine whether a driver was drowsy at the time of a crash.

Because of this serious problem, in order to make the driver aware before any such accident occurs, I have developed this program. It detects if a live human has had their eyes closed for more than a second. This sort of technology is useful for anybody interested in increased driving safety, including commercial & everyday drivers, car companies, and vehicle-insurance companies. You can check out the source code in here!

I started this project by collecting data (open and closed eyes) from a number of open-source datasets including the UMass Amherst's full facial dataset. I fine-tuned a pre-trained model with the collected dataset, which involved recreating the classifier layer and tuning the training hyperparameters. Once the model was prepared, I used OpenCV to capture video frames, and used them as the model's inputs to make a prediction about user's states. Finally, I wrote an honors thesis discussing the motivation, prior work, research procedure, and results of this project.
